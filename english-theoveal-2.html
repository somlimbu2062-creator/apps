Chapter 2: The Oval Portrait

Summary

The story begins with a wounded protagonist seeking refuge in a remote chateau in the Apennines. His valet secures a small, antique apartment for him in a remote turret. The room is decorated with tapestries, armorial trophies, and modern paintings. The protagonist, intrigued by the art, has his servant light candles and open the curtains so he can read a descriptive volume about the paintings and admire them.

As the night wears on, the protagonist adjusts the candelabrum to better light his book. In doing so, he discovers a portrait he hadn't noticed before—a captivating vignette of a young girl. The painting's lifelike quality is so striking that it both startles and frightens him. He becomes deeply contemplative, trying to understand the source of its powerful effect. Eventually, he turns to the volume to read about the painting's history.

The book reveals that the subject of the portrait was a woman of rare beauty who fell in love with and married the painter. The painter, consumed by his art, neglected everything else, including his new bride. He became obsessed with capturing her likeness, working feverishly day and night. Tragically, as he painted, her health and vitality drained away. The volume concludes with the painter, upon completing his masterpiece, exclaiming, "She was dead!" The protagonist falls back into bed, still captivated by the haunting portrait and its tragic tale.

Answer these questions.

a. Where did the narrator and his servant make forcible entrance?

The narrator and his servant made a forcible entrance into a desolate Chateau located in the Apennines of central Italy.

b. Which special picture did the narrator notice in the room?

The narrator noticed an oval-shaped picture of a young girl in the room.

c. Describe the portrait that the narrator saw in the room.

The portrait was in an oval-shaped frame and depicted the head and shoulders of a young woman. The arms, bosom, and even the ends of her hair seemed to fade into a vague, deep shadow that formed the background of the painting.

d. What is the relationship between the portrait painter and its subject?

The portrait painter and his subject were husband and wife.

In reference to the context

a. What is the central theme of the story? Who is the woman depicted in the oval portrait?

The central theme of "The Oval Portrait" is the conflict between art and life, and the idea that art can consume and destroy life. The woman depicted in the oval portrait is the beautiful young bride of the artist, whose life is sacrificed for the sake of his art.

b. "The Oval Portrait" is a short horror story by Edgar Allan Poe involving the disturbing circumstances surrounding a portrait in a chateau. Elaborate.

The story's horror lies in the supernatural and psychological unease it creates. The narrator's gradual discovery of the portrait's history—that the artist's obsession drained the life from his wife as he painted her—is deeply unsettling. The final reveal, when the artist completes the painting and realizes his wife is dead, is a shocking climax that underscores the macabre theme of art consuming life.

c. "The Oval Portrait" suggests that the woman's beauty condemns her to death. Discuss.

The story strongly suggests that the woman's beauty is her ultimate curse. Her beauty so captivated the artist that he became obsessed with immortalizing it on canvas. In his single-minded pursuit, he ignored her declining health, effectively sacrificing her life for his art. Her beauty is what made her a subject worthy of his obsessive attention, and it is this obsession that leads to her demise.

d. Discuss the story as a frame narrative (a story within a story).

"The Oval Portrait" is a perfect example of a frame narrative. The outer story is that of the wounded narrator seeking shelter in the chateau. He discovers the inner story, the tragic history of the portrait, by reading a book he finds. This structure builds suspense and mystery, as the narrator—and by extension, the reader—is led to a horrifying conclusion through a series of discoveries.

e. The story is told in a descriptive style, with plenty of imagery and symbolism. Which images and symbols do you find in the story?

The story is rich with imagery and symbolism. The chateau itself symbolizes decay and the past. The oval portrait represents the destructive power of art and obsession. The candelabrum symbolizes enlightenment and knowledge, as its repositioning reveals the hidden truth of the painting. The veil can be seen to symbolize the thin line between life and death.

f. What does the expression “She was dead!” mean?

The expression "She was dead!" is the horrifying climax of the story. It means that the woman in the portrait literally died at the exact moment the painter completed his final brushstroke. This statement reveals the ultimate price paid for the masterpiece and underscores the theme that the artist's obsession with art was a fatal drain on his wife's life.

Reference beyond the text

a. Do you think there is life in art?

The question of "life in art" is a philosophical one. From a literal standpoint, art is inanimate. However, figuratively, art can be said to have "life" in its ability to evoke emotions, tell stories, and preserve a moment in time. In the story, the painting's life-like quality is so potent that it seems to have literally absorbed the vitality of its subject, making it a powerful symbol of the consuming nature of art.

b. As a thing of art nothing could be more admirable than the painting itself. Explain.

This statement means that the painting is considered a flawless masterpiece from a purely artistic standpoint. Its technique, composition, and execution are beyond reproach. It is admired solely for its aesthetic qualities, separate from the tragic and disturbing story behind its creation. This highlights the artist's immense talent but also the moral ambiguity of his work.

c. A more intense look at the painting reveals the illusion. Have you noticed any such painting?

This idea is common in art criticism, where a painting that seems realistic from afar might reveal its nature as a collection of brushstrokes and pigment upon closer inspection. This concept is a core element of impressionism. A more intense look at the oval portrait reveals the "illusion" not of technique, but of the life it has captured—a life that was tragically given up to create it. This is a much darker and more sinister illusion than those found in most paintings.
